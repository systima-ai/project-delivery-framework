# Radical Candor — primer for the performance-conversation prep

PDF's performance-conversation workflow is built on Kim Scott's Radical Candor framework. This guide summarises the model and the practices that move difficult conversations toward the Radical Candor quadrant, which is where useful performance conversations happen.

## The 2×2

Two axes:

- **Care Personally** — the extent to which your behaviour signals you care about them as a person, not just as a producer.
- **Challenge Directly** — the extent to which you're willing to say the hard thing clearly.

The 2×2:

```
                    Challenge Directly (high)
                            |
                            |
   Obnoxious Aggression     |     Radical Candor
   (low care, high          |     (high care, high
    challenge)              |      challenge)
                            |
————————————————————————————+————————————————————————————
                            |
   Manipulative             |     Ruinous Empathy
   Insincerity              |     (high care, low
   (low care, low           |      challenge)
    challenge)              |
                            |
                    Challenge Directly (low)
```

(Care Personally axis: low on the left, high on the right.)

### Radical Candor (top right)

What you're aiming for. High care + high challenge. Behaviours:
- Direct feedback on specific behaviours
- Framing that signals "I'm telling you this *because* I care about your development / our relationship / the work succeeding"
- Tone calibrated to the person, not blunted by fear of upsetting them
- Real time given — not a drive-by comment in a corridor

### Ruinous Empathy (bottom right)

The most common failure mode for managers who think of themselves as "people people". High care, low challenge. Behaviours:
- Softening feedback to the point of meaninglessness
- "Sandwiching" hard messages between praise so the recipient doesn't notice the message
- Delaying difficult conversations indefinitely
- Allowing patterns to continue because raising them would be awkward

Symptom: the person being managed is genuinely surprised when consequences arrive — they thought everything was fine because that's what they were told.

### Obnoxious Aggression (top left)

Low care, high challenge. Behaviours:
- Direct feedback delivered in a context where the recipient feels judged, not supported
- Public criticism
- Tone that signals frustration rather than partnership
- "Being honest" as cover for not investing in the relationship

Symptom: the message lands but the relationship doesn't survive the delivery. Performance may improve briefly; trust erodes.

### Manipulative Insincerity (bottom left)

Low care, low challenge. The worst quadrant. Behaviours:
- Saying one thing to the person, another about them to others
- Withholding feedback to keep your own life easy
- Apparent agreement that masks lack of intent to act
- Triangulating through other channels (talking to HR before talking to the person)

Symptom: the manager has no genuine relationship with the person and isn't trying to build one; the work suffers; the person doesn't even know there's a problem until something formal happens.

## How to move toward Radical Candor

If your current quadrant is anything other than Radical Candor, the path forward is **specific behavioural shifts** in this conversation, not generic self-improvement.

### From Ruinous Empathy → Radical Candor

You're caring; you're under-challenging. The shift: name the specific behaviour, say it directly, accept the discomfort.

Concrete moves:
- **Open with intent stated.** "I want to talk about something I've been observing. It matters because [name the impact]."
- **One example, specific.** Pick the strongest specific example and stay focused on it. Many examples sound like accumulated grievance; one example sounds like analysis.
- **Don't sandwich.** Praise after hard feedback signals "this is awkward for me"; the recipient often doesn't hear the message.
- **Sit through the silence.** Don't fill it with reassurance. Their reaction is the conversation.

### From Obnoxious Aggression → Radical Candor

You're challenging; the care isn't landing. The shift: explicit care signals and tone work.

Concrete moves:
- **Lead with care, in your own words.** Not the corporate version ("I value your contribution"); the real version. "I want you to do well here, and I think you can, which is why I'm saying this."
- **In private.** No exceptions. Public challenge looks like aggression even when the substance is right.
- **Tone: low and slow.** If you'd say it the same way at the dinner table to someone you care about, the tone is probably right.
- **Ask before you tell.** "What's your read on how this went?" before "Here's what I observed."

### From Manipulative Insincerity → Radical Candor

This is the hardest shift because it usually signals you haven't built a real relationship with the person yet. The shift: invest in the relationship first; defer the conversation if necessary.

Concrete moves:
- **Have an actual relationship-building conversation first.** Not the performance conversation. Get to a place where you can credibly say "I care about how this lands for you."
- **Don't run the performance conversation through proxies.** No HR-first; no skip-level first; no email-without-a-meeting.
- **Be honest with yourself.** Are you avoiding this because the conversation will be hard, or because you don't actually care about the person's development? The answer changes the right move.

## The conversation itself: practical guide

### Opening (first 60 seconds)

The opening sets the quadrant. Three components:

1. **State the intent.** "I want to talk about [topic]. The reason is [impact]."
2. **Frame the care.** Brief; honest; in your own words. Not boilerplate.
3. **Invite their seat.** "Is now a good time?" or "Are you in the right head-space?" If not, reschedule — within 48h.

### The substance (5–15 minutes)

Use SBI structure:
- **Situation:** specific time and place. Not "in general", "always", "never".
- **Behaviour:** observable. What they did or didn't do. Not interpretation.
- **Impact:** consequence. For them, for the team, for the engagement.

One example, well-chosen. Multiple examples sound like a case being prosecuted.

### The pause (essential, often skipped)

After SBI, stop. Wait. Let them respond. Their response is the conversation.

Common manager failure here: filling the silence with softeners, qualifications, or "and another thing". Don't. The silence is doing the work.

### Their response — handle each type

- **Defensive.** Acknowledge the difficulty before re-stating. "I understand this isn't easy to hear. I still think it's worth getting right." Then re-state the SBI without softening it.
- **Agreeing.** Do not move on yet. Push for the commitment: "What do you want to do about it?" The commitment must come from them.
- **Deflecting.** Name the deflection gently. "I notice we've moved to [adjacent topic]. Can we come back to [the original]?"
- **Surprised.** Slow down. Give them time. Surprise often signals that prior feedback (yours or others') has been softened to the point of invisibility. Repair that pattern by being clearer now.

### The close

Three things in the close:

1. **Their commitment** in their words.
2. **Your commitment** (often: support, time, resources, follow-up).
3. **The follow-up date.** Specific. Not "let's check in soon".

### After

- **Document.** The workflow's "After the conversation" section is for this.
- **Tell whoever needs to know.** HR if pre-discussed; your own line manager if it affects them.
- **Hold the follow-up date.** Skipping the follow-up is the most common Ruinous Empathy slip.

## Common anti-patterns the workflow catches

- **"Performance issues" without a specific Situation.** Generality is the failure mode.
- **Multiple examples.** Pick one strong example.
- **Sandwiching.** Praise → criticism → praise is a Ruinous Empathy pattern.
- **No follow-up date.** Unenforced conversations don't change behaviour.
- **HR-first.** If HR is involved before the person knows, the conversation has already shifted quadrants.
- **Self-talk passing for prep.** "I just want to be honest with them" is not prep; it's a feeling. The workflow demands SBI and the rest.

## When this isn't the right workflow

- If the topic is **general 1:1** material → use `pdf-prep-1on1`.
- If the conversation is **a verdict, not a conversation** (you've already decided to exit them and are telling them) → that's a process, not a Radical Candor conversation. Engage HR; the workflow's framing won't apply.
- If you don't actually have a relationship with the person → repair that first; this workflow won't substitute for the missing relationship.
